What Makes Magic: The Gathering Cards Expensive?

Published

March 18, 2026

Magic: The Gathering Cards and Price

Magic: The Gathering has been around since 1993 and has produced thousands of unique cards. Similar to Pokemon, in addition to Magic fans just playing with the cards, there is also a large culture of card collecting. With that, some common and undesirable cards are worth pennies, while others are worth thousands of dollars. As a fan of the game, I wanted to dig into what actually drives those prices. Are expensive cards old? Rare? Banned for being too powerful? This project set out to answer exactly that.

Additionally, a newer way to play the game has recently turned traditional Magic: The Gathering playstyles on its head and heavily changed gameplay metas. This format, called Commander, is a 100 card format without any repeat cards allowed, which differs drastically from the 60 card formats with repeating cards allowed. This led me to look at an additional dimension for the cards and see how being legal in Commander affects price.

Getting the Data

I used the Scryfall API to collect card data. Scryfall is a free, open API that requires no account or API key, which makes it a fantastic resource for hobbyist data projects. I queried for all English, non-digital cards with a listed USD price, handling each page of 175 cards automatically until I had the full dataset. To be an ethical data scientist, I added a 100ms delay between requests as requested by the Scryfall API. Additionally, Scryfall’s terms of service explicitly permit this kind of personal and educational use, so no ethical concerns there.

From the raw data I kept only the fields relevant to my question: card name, price, foil price, colors, converted mana cost (CMC), Commander legality, rarity, card type, and release year. I then filtered down to the 1,000 most expensive cards by non-foil USD price, excluding basic lands (very common cards that are constantly reprinted) and oversized novelty cards (not legal in any form of the game) along the way. Prices ranged from $11.86 (Watery Grave) at the bottom to $5,142.02 (Timetwister) at the top.

What I Found

Age Matters A Lot

The cards from 1993, Magic’s first year, had an average price of $820.01, dwarfing every other year in the dataset. This finding was to be expected. With many of these first year cards being highly sought after and valuable, the creators of Magic: The Gathering made something called the Reserved List. The Reserved List is a set of cards that will never be reprinted, created to increase the value of older cards and make them more of a trophy to collectors. Many cards from the first set ever released are on the Reserved List, so this era being insanely expensive on average makes complete sense. In addition to this, early cards were printed in much smaller quantities, making them even more collectible. More recent years average between $20 and $40, reflecting higher print volume, more available supply, and less collectibility.

Banned Cards Are Expensive

In the 1,000 most expensive cards in Magic, there were a total of 19 that are banned in Commander. These 19 cards had an average price of $1,046.29, compared to just $66.20 for legal cards. There are several factors influencing why a banned card might be so expensive despite being banned in the most popular way to play. First, some of these cards are banned because there are so few and they are so powerful that banning them prevents the game from becoming a pay-to-win situation. Additionally, popular powerful cards that are not banned are often reprinted in future sets to keep them available for players, driving their cost down.

Colorless Cards Are King

In addition to simple availability, I wanted to explore how the color of a card relates to its price. In Magic there are 5 colors that define archetypes in the game: blue, white, red, green, and black, plus a colorless category. Cards have colors to describe their archetypes, and in Commander you are limited to certain archetypes based on the color of the card heading your deck. Cards can also have multiple colors, expanding the archetype they fall into but often limiting their playability in certain decks. Given this, colorless cards (all of which were artifacts and lands) had the highest average price at $131.35, well above any single color. Among colors, Blue came out on top at $99.10, followed by Black at $66.69. Red was the cheapest at $42.12. Colorless cards are playable in any deck regardless of the commander chosen, which likely drives up demand significantly.

Foil Cards Nearly Double in Price

In Magic: The Gathering, cards can be printed in a special foil treatment that gives them a shiny, reflective finish. Foil cards are rarer than their standard counterparts and can be highly sought after by collectors who want the most premium version of a card. Of the 1,000 cards in the dataset, 549 had a listed foil price. On average, foil versions cost $59.48 compared to $27.68 for non-foil, over a 2x multiplier and an average premium of +$31.80.

CMC Has Little Effect

Converted mana cost, or CMC, refers to the total amount of mana required to cast a card. In Magic, mana is the resource used to play cards each turn, and higher CMC cards are generally more powerful but harder to cast. Despite this, CMC showed very little correlation with price in this dataset. Cards with a CMC of 0 (mostly artifacts and lands that cost no mana to play) had the highest average price at $231.20. This makes sense also because cards with 0 CMC are also colorless, which is inherently expensive. Beyond a CMC of 0 the relationship is inconsistent. Every other CMC between 1 and 15 mana had a price between $13 and $90. This suggests that price is driven much more by scarcity, age, and demand than by how powerful or complex a card is to cast.
